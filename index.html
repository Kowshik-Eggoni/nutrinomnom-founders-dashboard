20g.nutrinomnom.com — Shelf-Stable Products
What's Working, What Isn't
Pan-India D2C · Shopify Hydrogen · 3 products at ₹399/250g
The Good "No new habits" positioning

You're not asking someone to change their life — you're telling them their existing life can do more. That's a fundamentally easier sell than any supplement brand. The protein gap framing (35g actual vs. 60g needed) creates the problem before introducing the solution. Textbook.

The Good The Math comparison section

₹64/serving podi vs. ₹120/serving whey protein is one of the most effective value-justification tools at this stage. It answers the price objection before the customer can even form it.

The Good Ingredient transparency

Showing exact percentages (Defatted Peanut Flour 47%, Whey 21%) is a genuine differentiator. Most brands hide behind "natural flavours." You're showing everything. That builds real trust with health-conscious buyers.

The Good Founder's story is honest

"I was buying protein powder and hating every sip. Then I realised — I eat podi rice pretty much every day." That's real. It works because it's specific and it doesn't try too hard. Keep this energy across everything you write.

The Bad "Hyderabad" in the hero

The hero eyebrow reads "🌱 By NutriNomNom · Hyderabad" — but you ship pan-India. The moment someone in Pune or Delhi sees "Hyderabad," a small subconscious friction is created. Is this local? Will it ship here? Move the Hyderabad mention to the story section where it's an authenticity marker, not a geographic limiter.

The Bad ₹349 vs. ₹399 inconsistency

The bottom CTA section says "Try any 20g product for ₹349" but every product is priced at ₹399. Which is it? This small inconsistency creates a real moment of distrust at the most critical point — just before someone decides to buy.

The Bad No subscribe-and-save

You're selling a daily consumable. Someone eating podi every day finishes 250g in under 2 weeks. Yet the entire site is optimised for a first order, with zero repeat-purchase architecture. No subscription toggle, no bundle discount, no re-order nudge. This is significant missed LTV.

The Bad Thin social proof

Three testimonials with initial-letter avatars, from three different cities, with perfectly smooth copy — they're not obviously fake, but they don't feel obviously real. No review count, no star aggregate, no UGC, no "x happy customers" number. For a food product, proof needs to work harder.

The Ugly Free shipping threshold kills trial

Free shipping above ₹999 means a single-product first-time buyer at ₹399 pays for shipping. To qualify, they need to buy 2.5 units. That's a huge ask from someone who hasn't tasted the product yet. Most early-stage D2C brands offer free shipping on first orders or set a ₹499 threshold.

The Ugly No FSSAI number visible

The trust strip says "Lab Tested" but there's no certificate link. FSSAI number is not shown anywhere on the site. For health-conscious urban buyers who read labels, this is a trust gap. It signals you might have something to hide — even if you don't.

The Ugly Zero connection to the parent brand

A Hyderabad customer finishes their podi, loves the brand, and goes back to the site. There's no link to the meal subscription. No "Also, we cook fresh meals in Hyderabad." No cross-pollination at all. Every day without this is a high-LTV customer you're leaving on the table.

The Ugly "Coming Soon" bar weakens the catalog

The greyed-out Protein Bar tile in the hero creates curiosity, yes — but for a new visitor who's never heard of you, it also signals "small startup, still figuring things out." Consider removing it or replacing it with a proper waitlist CTA so you're capturing intent instead of just showing a gap.

nutrinomnom.com — Meal Subscriptions
What's Working, What Isn't
Hyderabad-only · WordPress · WhatsApp-first conversion · ₹1565–₹25,000/plan
The Good Customer love is genuinely real

The testimonials here are some of the most credible on any early-stage site: full names, specific details ("4 kg while going to the gym daily"), and honest mixed feedback ("one curry with a sour taste"). That honesty is rare and builds more trust than polished five-star quotes.

The Good RDA methodology is a real differentiator

Most meal delivery says "healthy." You say: we use Recommended Dietary Allowance methodology, gather your biometrics, balance macros and micros. That's a real scientific process that separates you from a tiffin service. It deserves to be front and center, not buried in a paragraph.

The Bad Premium service, template website

At ₹12,500/month for the full plan, you're selling a premium product. Your customer is comparing you against a cook, a restaurant, and cooking themselves. The website looks like a default WordPress blog. There is zero visual expression of the care, freshness, and expertise that the copy describes.

The Bad Confusing pricing display

Pricing shows two numbers per plan (e.g., ₹1565 / ₹3130 for 1 week of lunch). It's never explained what the higher number means. If that's a 50% discount, shout it loudly. If it's not a real discount, remove the second number. Ambiguity here creates doubt right at the decision moment.

The Ugly WhatsApp-only blocks cold traffic

WhatsApp works for warm referrals. But for someone discovering you via Google or Instagram at 11pm, a WhatsApp CTA is a conversion wall. People don't want to initiate a conversation to buy something. They want to see a price, click, and pay. Every inbound visitor who doesn't send that WhatsApp message is a lost customer.

The Ugly Indistinguishable from a tiffin service

Cover the logo and show this website to 20 people. Zero would identify it as a premium, science-backed nutrition service. The NutriNomNom personality, the warmth, the food-as-care philosophy — all absent from the design. The mascot logo doesn't even appear on this site.

Strategic Questions
These aren't action items — they're forks in the road. Each one shapes what gets built next.
⚠️ These questions don't need to be answered today, but they need to be answered deliberately. Letting them stay ambiguous means the brand drifts rather than grows.

1 Is NutriNomNom expanding the meal subscription beyond Hyderabad?

If yes: the nutrinomnom.com site needs to change now — city selector, delivery zone map, waitlist for other cities. If no: that's a valid choice, but lean into Hyderabad hard. Make it a hyperlocal brand that dominates one city before replicating the model. The current site doesn't reflect either decision — it's neither pan-India-ready nor hyper-Hyderabad.

📍 Shapes: Site architecture, marketing budget, ops capacity, brand messaging

2 What is the role of the NutriNomNom mascot and the candy-color palette?

The design guide shows a playful, bright, mascot-driven brand. But 20g is clean, structured, and data-driven. These are two different brand personalities. The mascot suggests broad consumer appeal — families, kids, supermarket shelves. Is that the direction? Or is 20g the future and NutriNomNom becomes the holding company brand that stays behind the scenes? You can't be both simultaneously without confusing the market.

📍 Shapes: Brand guide, packaging design, social media tone, target audience

3 Who exactly is the 20g customer at 10x scale?

Right now the site speaks to health-aware urban millennials (25–35) who already know they need more protein. That's a well-defined, educated early adopter. But is that the segment to go deep on, or is there a broader play — homemakers who want to quietly upgrade their family's nutrition, older adults managing health conditions, office cafeterias? The "no new habits" positioning works for all of them, but the creative execution needs to pick one to start.

📍 Shapes: Ad targeting, content strategy, product development roadmap, distribution channels

4 Is "20g" a product line or a standalone brand?

Currently it's presented as "20g by NutriNomNom" — a sub-brand. But the 20g domain, the separate site, the distinct visual identity, and the pan-India ambition all feel more like a standalone brand that happens to share a founding story. This matters for: investor conversations (what are you building?), retailer conversations (which brand goes on shelf?), and customer conversations (which name do they remember?). Make this call deliberately.

📍 Shapes: Brand architecture, packaging, retailer/investor pitch, long-term product strategy

5 When does nutrinomnom.com get a real checkout?

This is framed as a question because it's really a resource prioritization call. Building a proper subscription checkout is 2–4 weeks of developer work. Is that the right use of time right now vs. growing the 20g D2C channel, or adding a new meal sub city, or hiring a chef? There's no wrong answer — but the longer you go without it, the more inbound traffic you're losing silently. Set a date for when this happens.

📍 Shapes: Conversion rate, ops workflow, scalability of the meal subscription business
